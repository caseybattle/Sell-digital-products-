# ANXIOUS GIRL'S BOUNDARIES PLAYBOOK
## 25 Scripts for Every Situation You've Been Dreading

*For anyone who knows what they need to say but can't find the words when it matters.*

---

## BEFORE YOU START: WHY THIS IS SO HARD FOR YOU SPECIFICALLY

You're not bad at boundaries. You have anxiety.

Here's what happens in your nervous system when you try to set a boundary:

1. You sense the possibility of disappointing someone
2. Your amygdala (the brain's alarm system) treats this like a physical threat
3. Your body floods with cortisol — the stress hormone
4. Your pre-frontal cortex (where boundaries live) goes partially offline
5. You either freeze, fawn (over-apologize, over-explain), or flee the situation

This isn't weakness. It's neuroscience. Your brain learned somewhere along the way that people's displeasure equals danger. So it protects you by making boundary-setting feel impossible.

**The script approach works because it bypasses this system.** You're not trying to find words in a high-cortisol moment. You've already found the words. You just deliver them.

---

## THE 4 BOUNDARY TYPES YOU'RE LIKELY VIOLATING

### 1. TIME Boundaries
You over-commit, say yes when you mean no, and then feel resentful. You're always "so busy" but never sure how you got that way.

*Signs you need this:* You feel exhausted by your calendar. You agree to things and immediately feel dread.

### 2. ENERGY Boundaries
You absorb other people's emotions. You leave conversations drained. You're the person everyone calls with their problems — and you can never say "I don't have capacity for this right now."

*Signs you need this:* You feel responsible for other people's feelings. You manage people's emotions so they don't get upset with you.

### 3. EMOTIONAL Boundaries
You share too much too fast, or nothing at all. You allow people to dump on you without reciprocity. You tolerate disrespect to keep the peace.

*Signs you need this:* You feel invaded by other people's problems. OR you feel like no one knows the real you.

### 4. DIGITAL Boundaries
You're always available. You respond to messages instantly even when you shouldn't. You can't turn off work after hours or disconnect from drama.

*Signs you need this:* You check your phone within 5 minutes of waking up. You feel anxious when you haven't responded to a message.

## THE BOUNDARY BUILDER FRAMEWORK

Use this before any difficult conversation:

**Step 1 — Name what happened (observation, not accusation)**
"When [specific behavior] happens..."

**Step 2 — Name how it affects you (impact)**
"...I feel [emotion], and it affects me by [consequence]."

**Step 3 — Name what you need (request)**
"Going forward, I need [specific change]."

**Step 4 — Name what happens if nothing changes (consequence, optional)**
"If that doesn't work for you, I'll need to [your action]."

*Example:* "When you call me after 10pm to talk through your problems, I feel overwhelmed and I can't sleep well afterward. Going forward, I need us to keep our hard conversations to before 9pm. If that's not possible, I may not always be able to answer."

## THE 25 SCRIPTS

### CATEGORY 1: WORK & PROFESSIONAL BOUNDARIES (Scripts 1–8)

---

**Script 1: Declining extra work you don't have capacity for**

*The situation:* Your manager or colleague asks you to take on something beyond your current workload.

**Say:** "I want to make sure I do this well. Right now I'm working on [X, Y, Z], and I don't have the bandwidth to add this without something else getting delayed. Can we talk about what to deprioritize, or is there a later timeline that would work?"

*Why this works:* You're not saying no to the work. You're saying no to doing it poorly. This reframes your boundary as professional quality control — not laziness.

*Don't say:* "I'm so sorry, I'm just really overwhelmed right now, but I can try to squeeze it in somehow..." (This leaves the door open for them to pressure you.)

---

**Script 2: Responding to an unreasonable deadline**

*The situation:* Someone needs something "by end of day" that would take two days to do well.

**Say:** "I can get you a solid version by [realistic date]. If you need something by today, it'll be a rough draft and I'd need to revisit it — which one works better for your timeline?"

*Why this works:* You're giving them control over the quality-vs-speed trade-off. You're not the obstacle. The deadline is.

*Don't say:* "I'll do my best!" and then panic for the rest of the day.

---

**Script 3: Ending a meeting that's run over**

*The situation:* A meeting has gone 15 minutes past its scheduled end and shows no sign of stopping.

**Say:** "I have a hard stop at [time] — I want to make sure we don't leave anything unresolved. Can we identify the top two action items and schedule a follow-up for the rest?"

*Why this works:* "Hard stop" is professional language that doesn't require explanation. You're also being helpful by protecting the group's time, not just your own.

*Don't say:* [Sitting silently, increasingly anxious, waiting for someone else to end it]

---

**Script 4: When a colleague consistently interrupts you in meetings**

*The situation:* Someone talks over you regularly. You've noticed the pattern.

**Say (in the moment):** "I wasn't finished — let me just complete my thought." [Continue without pausing for permission.]

**Say (privately, after):** "I've noticed a few times in meetings that I get cut off before I'm done. I don't think you're doing it on purpose, but it's been affecting my ability to contribute. Can we be more intentional about that?"

*Why this works:* The in-the-moment phrase is calm, direct, and requires no permission. The private conversation assumes positive intent while still naming the impact.

*Don't say:* "Oh, sorry, you go ahead." [And then never finish your thought]

---

**Script 5: Disconnecting after work hours**

*The situation:* You receive a work message or email at 8pm and feel the compulsion to respond.

**Say (to yourself):** "This can wait until 9am. If it were a true emergency, they would call."

**Say (to set the expectation proactively):** "Just so you know — I try not to check messages after 7pm. I'm always available during work hours and will respond first thing in the morning."

*Why this works:* You're not refusing to work. You're setting an expectation in advance so people aren't surprised by a delayed response.

*Don't say:* Nothing, and then resent your phone all evening.

---

**Script 6: When you can't attend a work event**

*The situation:* There's a "strongly encouraged" after-hours work event you genuinely cannot or do not want to attend.

**Say:** "I have a prior commitment that evening — I won't be able to make it. Hope it's a great event."

*Why this works:* "Prior commitment" is complete. It requires no elaboration. It is also true — your prior commitment to your own rest, family, or sanity is a real commitment.

*Don't say:* A three-paragraph explanation of why you can't come, ending with an apology.

---

**Script 7: Addressing a colleague who vents to you constantly**

*The situation:* A coworker treats you as their therapist. You care about them but you're depleted.

**Say:** "I hear you, and I want to support you. I have to be honest — I'm not in the headspace to be really present for this today. Can we talk later in the week when I can give it the attention it deserves?"

*Why this works:* You're not abandoning them. You're requesting a better time. This is more respectful than half-listening while internally screaming.

*Don't say:* [Nodding, saying "mmm" and "wow that's so hard" for 45 minutes while your cortisol spikes]

---

**Script 8: When you're asked to do something outside your job description**

*The situation:* Someone asks you to do work that isn't your job.

**Say:** "That's outside of what I typically handle — I want to make sure it gets to the right person. Who would be the best owner for this?"

*Why this works:* You're being helpful, not difficult. You're not saying "not my job" — you're routing it correctly. The boundary is implicit.

### CATEGORY 2: RELATIONSHIPS & SOCIAL BOUNDARIES (Scripts 9–17)

---

**Script 9: Declining social plans without guilt**

*The situation:* You've been invited to something you don't want to attend. You're tired. You need to recharge.

**Say:** "Thank you so much for thinking of me — I'm going to have to sit this one out. I hope you all have a great time."

*Why this works:* No reason required. "Sit this one out" is friendly and final. The well-wish shows you're not angry — you just can't come.

*Don't say:* A detailed explanation of your anxiety, your exhaustion, your introversion, your current mental load — followed by an apology.

---

**Script 10: Handling the "you've changed" pushback**

*The situation:* Someone who benefited from your old people-pleasing self notices you've started having boundaries. They don't love it.

**Say:** "You're right — I've been working on showing up more authentically. I understand if that takes some adjustment."

*Why this works:* You're not apologizing for changing. You're acknowledging it calmly and owning it. People who say "you've changed" often mean "you're not as easy to take advantage of anymore."

*Don't say:* "I'm so sorry, I don't mean to be different, I still care about you so much, I'm just going through a phase..." [And then abandon the boundary to reassure them]

---

**Script 11: Ending a phone call when you're done**

*The situation:* A conversation has run its course. You want to end it without being rude.

**Say:** "I'm going to let you go — I've got a few things I need to get to. So good talking to you."

*Why this works:* "Let you go" implies you're doing them a favor. It's the softest possible exit. You don't need a real reason to end a phone call.

---

**Script 12: Telling someone their advice is not helpful**

*The situation:* Someone keeps giving you unsolicited advice about a decision you've already made or a situation they don't understand.

**Say:** "I appreciate that you care. What would actually help me most right now is just [to vent / someone to listen / support without advice]. Can you do that?"

*Why this works:* You're redirecting, not rejecting. You're telling them exactly how to help instead of leaving them guessing — which usually leads to more unwanted advice.

---

**Script 13: Setting limits with a guilt-tripping family member**

*The situation:* A parent, sibling, or extended family member uses guilt to get what they want. ("After everything I've done for you..." / "I guess I just don't matter...")

**Say:** "I hear that you're upset. I love you, and I'm still not going to [do the thing]. When you're ready to talk about something else, I'm here."

*Why this works:* You're acknowledging their emotion without being moved by the manipulation. You're not engaging in the guilt loop. You're available — just not for that particular dynamic.

*Don't say:* "You're right, I'm so selfish, I'll do it."

---

**Script 14: When someone shares your private information**

*The situation:* Someone told others something you shared in confidence.

**Say:** "I need to tell you this bothered me. I shared that with you privately and I wasn't okay with it going further. I need to know I can trust you with personal things."

*Why this works:* You're naming the behavior, naming the impact, and naming what you need. The word "need" signals this is serious — not a complaint but a requirement.

---

**Script 15: Saying no to lending money**

*The situation:* A friend or family member asks to borrow money. You can't or don't want to.

**Say:** "I'm not in a position to lend money right now — I've had to get strict about that for my own finances. I hope you're able to find another way through this."

*Why this works:* "Not in a position" is complete without being a lie. "I've had to get strict about that" makes it a policy, not a personal rejection. The well-wish is genuine.

*Don't say:* "Oh, I wish I could, let me see, maybe I could scrape together..." [And then resent them for the next six months]

---

**Script 16: Addressing a friend who only calls when they need something**

*The situation:* You've noticed a pattern — they appear when they want support and disappear when things are good.

**Say:** "I've noticed I mostly hear from you when something is wrong. I care about you and I want to be there — I also want this to feel more mutual. Can we make a plan to just catch up sometime when things aren't hard?"

*Why this works:* You're naming the pattern without accusation. You're offering a path forward. You're asking for reciprocity, not punishing the one-sidedness.

---

**Script 17: Responding to an offensive comment from someone you care about**

*The situation:* A friend or family member says something that offends you. You want to address it without a big fight.

**Say:** "Hey — that landed weird for me. I don't think you meant anything by it, but can I tell you why it hit differently?"

*Why this works:* The assumption of positive intent disarms defensiveness. Asking permission to share ("can I tell you") makes them a willing participant instead of a cornered person.

### CATEGORY 3: ROMANTIC & DATING BOUNDARIES (Scripts 18–22)

---

**Script 18: Slowing things down when you're moving too fast**

*The situation:* Things are escalating (emotionally or physically) faster than feels right for you.

**Say:** "I'm really enjoying this. I also want to take it a bit slower so I can stay present with it. That's not a reflection on you — I just know myself."

*Why this works:* You're expressing genuine interest while setting the pace. "I know myself" signals self-awareness, not fear. It invites them to respect your process.

---

**Script 19: Expressing a need without an ultimatum**

*The situation:* Something your partner does (or doesn't do) is affecting you and you need to name it.

**Say:** "There's something I need to bring up because I care about us. When [specific thing] happens, I feel [feeling]. I'm not saying you're doing it wrong — I just need you to know it affects me. Can we talk about it?"

*Why this works:* "I care about us" frames it as a team conversation, not an attack. You're disclosing your experience, not accusing them of wrongdoing.

---

**Script 20: Addressing inconsistent behavior (hot and cold)**

*The situation:* Someone pursues you intensely and then pulls away. The pattern is disorienting.

**Say:** "I've noticed things feel inconsistent between us and I'm not sure how to read it. I'm not trying to pressure you — I just do better with some clarity. What's going on for you?"

*Why this works:* You're naming the pattern without accusation. You're acknowledging your own needs ("I do better with clarity") while opening space for them to explain.

---

**Script 21: Leaving a situationship cleanly**

*The situation:* You're in something undefined that's not serving you. You need out.

**Say:** "I've been doing a lot of thinking and I need to be honest with you. This arrangement isn't working for me anymore. I care about you and I need to take care of myself here. I think it's best if we end things."

*Why this works:* It's complete. It doesn't invite negotiation. It's not cruel, but it's final. "I need to take care of myself here" is a statement of necessity, not criticism of them.

*Don't say:* "I just feel like maybe we should like, I don't know, maybe take a break? Or like, see what happens?" [This is not a boundary. This is a question.]

---

**Script 22: Responding to "you're too sensitive"**

*The situation:* You expressed a feeling or set a boundary and got dismissed as overreacting.

**Say:** "I might be sensitive — and what I'm feeling is still real. I'm not asking you to agree that it makes sense. I'm asking you to hear that it's how I feel."

*Why this works:* You're not defending your sensitivity (which invites debate). You're redirecting to the emotional reality, which is non-negotiable.

---

### CATEGORY 4: PERSONAL & SELF BOUNDARIES (Scripts 23–25)

---

**Script 23: Responding to unsolicited advice about your body, appearance, or life choices**

*The situation:* Someone comments on your weight, your eating, your relationship status, your career, your parenting, or any other thing that is none of their business.

**Say:** "I'm good — I'm not looking for input on that."

*Why this works:* Short. Complete. No explanation owed. The phrase "I'm good" signals you're not upset; you just aren't accepting the input.

*Don't say:* [A 3-minute defensive explanation of your choices]

---

**Script 24: Telling someone you need space**

*The situation:* You're overwhelmed and need time alone to reset. Someone keeps seeking your attention.

**Say:** "I need a little time to myself to recharge — I'll reach out when I'm ready. It's not about you; it's just how I work."

*Why this works:* You're not ghosting. You're communicating. "I'll reach out when I'm ready" gives them a timeline. "It's not about you" is often both true and necessary to say.

---

**Script 25: Saying no without apologizing**

*The situation:* Someone asks you for something you cannot or do not want to do.

**Say:** "No, I won't be able to do that."

Or: "That doesn't work for me."

Or: "I'm going to pass on that."

*Why this works:* Because a complete sentence is complete. No sorry. No excuse. No over-explanation. The discomfort of saying no cleanly is 10 seconds long. The discomfort of saying yes when you mean no lasts weeks.

## MANAGING THE GUILT AFTERWARD

You said the boundary. They reacted. Now you feel terrible. Here's what to do with that.

**Recognize the guilt loop:**
Guilt → "I hurt them" → "I should take it back" → Abandon boundary → Relief (short-term) → Resentment (long-term) → Repeat

**Interrupt the loop by asking:**
1. Did I say something untrue? (If no → not your guilt to carry)
2. Did I say something cruel? (If no → not your guilt to carry)
3. Am I responsible for how they feel about my truth? (No. You're responsible for how you express it, not how they receive it.)

**Say to yourself:** "I told my truth kindly. The discomfort they feel is the cost of the adjustment. That's not the same as harm."

**The 24-hour rule:** Don't revise or apologize for a boundary within 24 hours of setting it. Most guilt is highest immediately after. It usually passes. If it's still there in 24 hours, revisit.
