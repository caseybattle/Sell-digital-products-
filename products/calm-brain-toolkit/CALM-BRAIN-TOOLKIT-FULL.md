# THE CALM BRAIN TOOLKIT
## CBT-Based Templates for Overthinking, People-Pleasing & the Brain That Won't Shut Off

---

## HOW TO USE THIS TOOLKIT

This is not a journal you fill out every day until you forget about it.

It's a toolkit — which means you pick up the right tool for what's happening right now.

- Brain spiraling before bed? → **Evening Wind-Down Routine**
- Stuck in an anxious thought loop? → **Thought Court Worksheet**
- Sunday dread creeping in? → **Sunday Reset Planner**
- Overwhelmed and need permission to stop? → **Permission to Rest Menu**
- Need to get the noise out of your head? → **Brain Dump to Clarity**

Use what you need. Leave what you don't.

---

## TOOL 1: BRAIN DUMP TO CLARITY
### *When your head is so full you can't function*

**How to use it:** Set a 10-minute timer. Write everything in your head into the prompts below. Don't edit. Don't organize. Just get it out.

---

**DUMP ZONE — What's taking up space in my head right now?**

*(Write everything: worries, to-dos, unfinished thoughts, things you're dreading, things you said yesterday that you're still thinking about, that email you need to send, the conversation you're rehearsing)*

[Free write space — fill completely]

---

**Now look at what you wrote. Sort each item:**

| Item | Category | Action |
|------|----------|--------|
| | URGENT / IN MY CONTROL | Do it or schedule it |
| | NOT URGENT / IN MY CONTROL | Add to a list, release it |
| | NOT IN MY CONTROL | See processing prompts below |
| | NOT REAL (catastrophizing) | Challenge it with Thought Court |

---

**For items "NOT IN MY CONTROL":**

What is the worst realistic outcome? _______________

What is the most likely outcome? _______________

What would I tell a close friend who was worried about this? _______________

What is the one thing I can control about this situation? _______________

---

**CLARITY STATEMENT** (fill in after the dump):

After clearing my head, the ONE thing that actually needs my attention today is:

_______________

Everything else can wait, doesn't exist yet, or isn't mine to carry.

## TOOL 2: THOUGHT COURT WORKSHEET
### *Put your anxious thought on trial*

**CBT principle:** Thoughts are not facts. This worksheet helps you examine a distressing thought the way a lawyer would examine evidence — not to dismiss your feelings, but to find out whether the thought holds up under scrutiny.

---

**THE ANXIOUS THOUGHT** (write it exactly as it appears in your head):

"_______________"

---

**STEP 1: IDENTIFY THE DISTORTION**

Which cognitive distortion is at work? (Check all that apply)

- [ ] **Catastrophizing** — Assuming the worst possible outcome
- [ ] **Mind reading** — Assuming you know what someone else is thinking
- [ ] **Fortune telling** — Predicting a negative future as if it's certain
- [ ] **All-or-nothing thinking** — No middle ground (always/never, perfect/failure)
- [ ] **Personalization** — Assuming you caused something that wasn't about you
- [ ] **Overgeneralization** — One bad thing = everything is bad
- [ ] **Emotional reasoning** — "I feel it, so it must be true"
- [ ] **Should statements** — Rigid rules about how you or others "should" be
- [ ] **Filtering** — Focusing only on the negative, ignoring positives
- [ ] **Labeling** — Applying a harsh label to yourself or others

---

**STEP 2: THE PROSECUTION** (evidence that the thought is TRUE)

List only real, factual evidence — not feelings, not interpretations:

1. _______________
2. _______________
3. _______________

---

**STEP 3: THE DEFENSE** (evidence that the thought is NOT TRUE or NOT CERTAIN)

1. _______________
2. _______________
3. _______________

*Prompts to help:*
- Has this feared outcome actually happened before?
- Are there other explanations for the situation?
- What would a neutral observer say?
- What's the evidence the opposite is true?

---

**STEP 4: THE VERDICT**

How true is the original thought on a scale of 0–100%? ______%

(Most anxious thoughts start at 90–100% certainty. After this process, most land at 30–50%.)

---

**STEP 5: THE BALANCED THOUGHT**

Write a new thought that's honest but not catastrophic. It doesn't have to be positive — just accurate.

"It's possible that _____________, AND it's also true that _____________. A more balanced view is _____________."

---

**How do you feel now?**

Rate your anxiety about this thought before and after:

Before: ___/10     After: ___/10

*Even a 2-point drop matters. That's your brain learning to regulate.*

## TOOL 3: THE 5 EVENING WIND-DOWN ROUTINES
### *For when your brain won't let you sleep*

**Instructions:** Pick one routine based on how much time you have. Do it in order. Don't skip steps — the sequence matters.

---

### ROUTINE A: THE 5-MINUTE RESET (Bare minimum)

**Step 1 — Body scan (1 min):** Close your eyes. Start at the top of your head. Slowly move your attention down through your face, jaw, neck, shoulders, chest, stomach, hips, legs, feet. Wherever you feel tension, take a breath into that spot and exhale.

**Step 2 — Tomorrow prep (2 min):** Write down the ONE most important thing you need to do tomorrow. Just one. Put the pen down.

**Step 3 — Permission statement (2 min):** Say out loud or write: "I've done enough today. What didn't get done today will still exist tomorrow. My body needs rest. I'm going to sleep."

---

### ROUTINE B: THE 15-MINUTE UNWIND

**Step 1 — Screen transition (2 min):** Close every screen. Put your phone face-down or in another room. This step is non-negotiable.

**Step 2 — Brain dump (5 min):** Write everything on your mind onto paper. Not organized — just out. Once it's on paper, your brain can release the job of holding it.

**Step 3 — Gratitude + one thing (3 min):** Write three things that were not terrible today. Then write the ONE thing that needs your attention tomorrow.

**Step 4 — Body release (5 min):** Lay down. Progressive muscle relaxation: tense each muscle group for 5 seconds, then release. Feet → calves → thighs → stomach → hands → arms → shoulders → face. By the time you reach your face, most people are already drowsy.

---

### ROUTINE C: THE 30-MINUTE DECOMPRESS (When the day was a lot)

**Step 1 — Environmental shift (5 min):** Change something physical — get into comfortable clothes if you haven't, make tea, take a warm shower or wash your face. A physical transition signals to your nervous system that the day is over.

**Step 2 — Feeling identification (5 min):** Don't suppress or perform. Name what you actually feel. "I'm anxious about tomorrow." "I'm frustrated about that conversation." "I feel guilty about not calling her back." Just name it — don't fix it.

**Step 3 — Thought Court for one thing (10 min):** If there's one thought looping, use the Thought Court worksheet above. Just one.

**Step 4 — Intentional reading or audio (10 min):** Something light and unrelated to your problems. Fiction, nature sounds, a calm podcast, ASMR. Your brain needs input that's interesting enough to prevent looping but calm enough to not stimulate.

**Note:** No news, no social media, nothing that requires you to form opinions or feel emotions about strangers' lives.

---

### ROUTINE D: THE ANXIETY SPIKE RESPONSE (When you wake up at 3am)

**Step 1 — Don't reach for your phone.** The urge is strong. Resist it.

**Step 2 — 4-7-8 breathing:** Breathe in for 4 counts → hold for 7 counts → exhale for 8 counts. Do this four times. The extended exhale activates your parasympathetic nervous system.

**Step 3 — Label it:** Say to yourself, "This is anxiety. It's 3am. My nervous system is activated. This will pass."

**Step 4 — If you must get up:** Write down what's in your head. Three sentences maximum. Then write: "I will handle this in the morning." Go back to bed.

---

### ROUTINE E: THE SUNDAY NIGHT VERSION (The Sunday Scaries)

**Step 1 — Name what you're dreading (5 min):** Be specific. Not "work" but "the meeting with [person] about [thing]." Not "everything" but the top 2–3 specific fears.

**Step 2 — Reality-check each one (10 min):** For each fear, answer: What's the worst realistic outcome? What's the most likely outcome? What's one thing I can do to prepare?

**Step 3 — Tomorrow's single priority (2 min):** What is the ONE thing that, if you do it, makes Monday a success? Write it down.

**Step 4 — Close the loop (3 min):** "I've thought about what I need to think about. The rest can wait. Sunday night is mine."

## TOOL 5: PERMISSION TO REST — THE SELF-CARE MENU
### *For when you need to stop but can't stop judging yourself for stopping*

**The premise:** You've internalized the idea that rest must be earned. It hasn't been. Rest is maintenance, not reward. You don't need to deserve it.

**How to use this:** When you feel the pull to push through exhaustion, guilt, or overwhelm — open this menu. Pick one thing from the time block that matches your window. Do it without your phone.

---

### 2-MINUTE STOPS
*(When you can't stop for long)*

- Close your eyes and take 5 deep breaths. Count them. That's it.
- Step outside and look at something that isn't a screen.
- Drink a full glass of water slowly, standing still.
- Stretch your arms above your head, hold for 30 seconds. Release.
- Name 5 things you can see right now. Slowly.

---

### 10-MINUTE RESETS
*(A real break)*

- Walk around the block with no podcast, no music, no agenda.
- Make something warm to drink and sit without looking at your phone.
- Lie flat on the floor. Stare at the ceiling. Do nothing for 8 minutes.
- Do one gentle stretch sequence: neck rolls, shoulder rolls, forward fold, child's pose.
- Write three sentences about what you're feeling right now. Don't try to fix it.

---

### 30-MINUTE RESTORES
*(When you're running on fumes)*

- Take a bath or shower with no rushed agenda.
- Watch one episode of something comforting — comfort TV only, nothing intense.
- Read for 30 minutes. Not news. Fiction or something unrelated to your problems.
- Sit outside somewhere and do nothing. Observe. Don't create content about it.
- Nap. Actually nap. Set a 25-minute timer.

---

### FULL-EVENING RESETS
*(When you're past the edge)*

- Declare the evening a no-obligation zone. Cancel what you can. Keep what you must.
- Order food or cook something simple. Don't try to be impressive.
- Put on comfortable clothes before 6pm.
- Watch comfort TV, read, take a bath, go to bed early. Not all of these — pick one.
- Tell someone "I'm having a hard week and I need to be offline tonight." Then be offline.

---

### THE GUILT INTERRUPT

When the voice says "you should be doing something productive right now," respond:

**"Resting IS doing something. I am maintaining the only body and brain I have. I can't pour from empty. I'm choosing to refill."**

Write that on a sticky note and put it somewhere you'll see it.

## 20 JOURNALING PROMPTS FOR ANXIOUS MINDS

Use these when you need to process something and don't know where to start.

1. What am I actually afraid of right now, in the most specific terms possible?
2. What would I tell a close friend who was feeling exactly what I'm feeling?
3. What thought keeps coming back to me this week? What is it trying to protect me from?
4. What have I been avoiding, and what do I think will happen if I face it?
5. What am I pretending is fine when it's not?
6. Where in my body do I feel this anxiety? What does it feel like physically?
7. What would "enough" look like today — not perfect, just enough?
8. Whose voice is the harshest critic in my head? What do they say? What would I say back?
9. What am I grateful for right now that I usually take for granted?
10. What would I do today if I wasn't afraid of what people thought?
11. What have I been saying yes to that I actually meant no to?
12. What is one kind thing I could do for myself today that doesn't require anyone else?
13. What do I need more of right now? Less of?
14. If this situation resolved itself, what would life look like? How can I get closer to that?
15. What am I holding onto that doesn't belong to me anymore?
16. Where am I being too hard on myself about something that was simply hard?
17. What story am I telling myself about this situation? Is it the only possible story?
18. What does my body need right now that I've been ignoring?
19. If past me could see present me, what would they be proud of?
20. What is one true thing about myself that anxiety makes me forget?
